Final Project Track A: Kemper Hall Indoor and Outdoor Tracker

DRAFT — for student review only. Requirements, deadlines, and rubric details may change before finalization. The overall scope and deliverables are stable enough for team formation and early planning.

At a Glance

In one sentence. Build a MATLAB app that tracks a walk from outdoors (using GPS) to indoors (using your phone's accelerometer) through all four floors of Kemper Hall, then uploads the walk to a cloud service so you can load and replay it later.

What stays from Mini-Projects 4 and 5. Your SensorDataAnalyzer class, your SensorDashboard.mlapp, and the separation-of-concerns discipline. You extend these; you do not rewrite them.

What is new. A live map of your walk drawn from GPS data, automatic switching between "outdoor" and "indoor" modes based on GPS signal quality, indoor floor detection from accelerometer patterns, and cloud upload and replay through ThingSpeak.

Core deliverable. A working app that records one complete walk, uploads to ThingSpeak, and reads back a past walk; a short Error Characterization Report (2 to 3 pages) showing how accurate your app was on the standard Kemper Evaluation Route; and a 6-minute YouTube video following the format in the Assessment Guide.

Project Description

Starting outdoors on the UC Davis campus, you track a walk using GPS and phone sensors. As you approach and enter Kemper Hall, the GPS signal degrades and your app automatically switches to an indoor mode that uses accelerometer-derived information to continue tracking the walk across all four floors. At the end of the walk, your app uploads a summary of the route to a shared cloud database (ThingSpeak), and the app can read past walks back later to display and replay them.

The project builds directly on your Mini-Projects. Your Mini-Project 4 class SensorDataAnalyzer is the starting point for the analysis layer; your Mini-Project 5 app SensorDashboard is the starting point for the GUI. You will extend both. You are not expected to rebuild either from scratch.

In addition to App Designer skills from MP5, this project will expose you to the following additional topics:

  • Visualizing geospatial data on a live map using geoaxes and geoplot.
  • Running a live-updating GUI during continuous sensor acquisition.
  • Detecting physical events (stair climbing, elevator use) from accelerometer signatures.
  • Uploading structured data to a cloud platform (ThingSpeak) and reading it back.
  • Characterizing sensor error against ground truth in a known environment.

Why This Project

In Mini-Project 4 you watched dead reckoning fail. You walked forward and back, expecting a final displacement near zero, and your integrated accelerometer estimate drifted off instead. The optional challenge at the end of MP4 invited improvements, but no pure-accelerometer fix makes the drift go away in principle. Real fitness trackers and car navigation systems solve this by pairing accelerometer data with an absolute position source (like GPS). In this project you do exactly that: GPS outdoors, honest accelerometer-based indoor handling once GPS is no longer available, and error measurement against a known route so you learn to report engineering results with numbers rather than adjectives.

Project Requirements

Your project is divided into required core functionality, a required reach feature, and a team-chosen reach feature.

Core Functionality (Must-Have)

The following functionality is required:

  1. A reasonably attractive user interface that extends SensorDashboard.mlapp from Mini-Project 5. You may rename, rearrange, and add components freely. The MP5 separation-of-concerns principle must be preserved: data acquisition, analysis, and cloud communication live in classes, not in callback bodies. Callbacks may be brief orchestration only.

  2. Start and Stop controls. A Start Activity button begins acquiring GPS (latitude, longitude, altitude, speed, horizontalAccuracy) and accelerometer data simultaneously from mobiledev. A corresponding Stop Activity button ends data collection and finalizes the workout.

  3. Live outdoor view. While GPS is reporting a good fix, the app displays a map (using geoaxes and geoplot) with the walk path drawn as new GPS points arrive. The user's current position is kept visible in the view.

  4. Automatic regime switch. The app monitors the GPS horizontal accuracy returned by poslog. When the reported accuracy degrades past a threshold your team chooses (typical indoor values are 30 meters or worse), the app switches its main display from the outdoor map to an indoor floor-plan view. When outdoor accuracy returns, the app switches back. The current regime (Outdoor GPS, Indoor Sensors) is visible to the user at all times.

  5. Floor detection indoors. The app reports which floor of Kemper Hall the user is currently on (1 through 4). Floor changes are detected from accelerometer patterns: stair climbing and elevator rides each leave distinctive signatures (see Appendix A). You start the walk on the floor the user selects; the floor counter increments or decrements as transitions are detected.

  6. Indoor position estimate. While indoors, the app overlays an estimated (x, y) position on a floor-plan image of the current floor. The estimate may be as simple as a step counter combined with a fixed stride length and the phone's orientation (azimuth). You are not expected to achieve commercial-grade accuracy; you are expected to produce a functional estimate and characterize its error (see required reach below).

  7. Live time-series chart. One chart updates continuously during the walk. Your team chooses the signal: speed from GPS, resultant acceleration, step cadence, or altitude. Only one chart is required in core.

  8. Live summary panel. A small panel updates continuously with at least three numbers: current regime, elapsed workout time, and total distance (sum of GPS-segment distances outdoors plus step-count times stride-length indoors).

  9. ThingSpeak upload. At the end of a workout, the app pushes a decimated summary of the walk to a team ThingSpeak channel. Appendix C describes the field layout. At a minimum the channel stores one record per 15 seconds of wall-clock time containing position, regime, floor, speed, and accelerometer magnitude.

  10. Load and replay. The app can read a past walk from ThingSpeak (by selecting from a list of recorded walks) and replay the path on the map and floor plan.

Required Reach Feature: Error Characterization Report

Your team walks the standard Kemper Hall Evaluation Route (Appendix B) once, then uses your own app to record and analyze the walk. Your report documents:

  • Return-to-start error. The walk returns to the same outdoor starting point. What does your app estimate the final displacement to be?
  • Floor detection accuracy. At each of the route's indoor checkpoints, what floor did your app report? How many were correct?
  • GPS handoff location. Where, geographically, did your app's regime switch occur? Was the handoff clean or ambiguous?
  • One concrete improvement you did not implement. Describe an engineering change that would reduce one of the errors above, and explain mechanistically why it would help.

The deliverable is a short technical memo (2 to 3 pages) with at least one figure showing your app's recorded path overlaid on the actual route.

Chosen Reach Feature: Pick One

You select one additional reach feature in addition to core and the error report. Your choice should give your project its individual flavor. We will consider the creativity and complexity of your reach goal when evaluating your project. Some suggestions:

  • Live streaming to ThingSpeak. The app pushes a new record every 15 seconds during the walk, not only at the end. Anyone with the channel URL can watch the walk on the ThingSpeak web view.
  • Accelerometer bias subtraction. Before the walk begins, the app samples the phone at rest for 2 seconds and computes the mean accelerometer reading. During the walk, that mean is subtracted before further processing. Document whether step detection and indoor position estimation improve measurably.
  • Activity classifier. The app classifies each moment of the walk into one of four states (stationary, walking, stairs, elevator) using accelerometer features (variance, peak rate). Classifications are displayed on the live panel and saved with the walk record.
  • Pause and Resume. The user can pause a walk mid-route; paused intervals do not contribute to path, distance, or statistics. Resuming continues the walk from the current sensor state.
  • Route replay animation. When a saved walk is loaded, a marker moves along the path at adjustable playback speed (for example, 1x, 2x, 5x) with synchronized updates to the summary panel.
  • GPX export. Completed walks export to a standard .gpx file that Strava or Garmin Connect can import. Demonstrate the export by successfully importing one of your walks.
  • Weather snapshot. At the start of each walk, the app queries a public weather API (Open-Meteo requires no API key) and records temperature, wind, and air quality to the walk record. The history view displays weather alongside other stats.
  • Splits. The walk is subdivided automatically at user-defined intervals (every 500 m, or every 2 minutes). Per-split time, distance, mean speed, and floor changes appear in a dynamically generated panel.

Optional Stretch Challenge (Ungraded)

For teams that finish the required work early and want to address the MP4 drift problem directly: implement GPS-anchored correction. Every time a new GPS fix arrives with good accuracy, snap the indoor position estimate back to the GPS position; between fixes, the indoor estimate uses the accelerometer-derived offsets. This is not a Kalman filter; it is "trust GPS when available, use accelerometer to fill gaps." Teams that complete this challenge should document it in their video and in the Error Characterization Report with quantitative accuracy results. A MathWorks-provided prize may be available for the most accurate implementation, based on the reported results.

Notes on Scope

  • Outdoor testing is required. Plan multiple Kemper walks during development. Do not schedule your first walk the week of the demo.
  • Keep the UI responsive. Acquiring two sensor streams plus uploading to ThingSpeak plus live plotting stresses the MATLAB single-threaded event loop. Use a timer object (Appendix D) that fires once per second or less, batches the sensor reads, and redraws the plots. Do not try to plot at raw sensor rates.
  • Assume GPS accuracy of 3 to 10 meters outdoors on open campus. Design around that. Do not set internal acceptance criteria that require sub-meter GPS.
  • Indoor position will drift. This is not a bug; it is a consequence of the sensors you have. The required reach feature asks you to measure the drift, not eliminate it.
  • ThingSpeak rate limits apply even on the paid subscription. Plan around one update per second per channel. Decimate on the app side before uploading.

Appendix A: Sensor Reality

GPS on a smartphone

Typical horizontal accuracy is 3 to 10 meters outdoors under open sky on a modern phone. Under trees, near tall buildings, or near a Kemper Hall entrance, accuracy degrades to 20 meters or worse. Indoors, GPS is effectively unusable; the phone falls back to Wi-Fi and cell-tower trilateration, which on campus typically reports 50 meters or more of error. The field poslog returns named horizontalAccuracy is the phone's own estimate of its accuracy in meters; it is the right quantity to threshold on for your regime switch.

Accelerometer signatures indoors

Stair climbing. Walking up or down stairs produces vertical acceleration peaks at roughly 1 to 2 Hz (one peak per step), superimposed on a slight sustained vertical offset from the body's lifting motion. Compared to flat walking, the vertical peaks are larger and the horizontal peaks are smaller. Counting vertical peaks during stair climbing gives you stair count; dividing by the stairs-per-floor count for Kemper gives you floor transitions.

Elevator rides. An elevator starting, cruising, and stopping has a very distinctive vertical acceleration profile: a brief sustained non-zero acceleration (about 0.5 to 1 m/s² for 1 to 3 seconds) when starting, near-zero during cruise (gravity only), and a matching opposite deceleration at the end. The sign of the start acceleration tells you up or down. The duration of the cruise is roughly proportional to the number of floors traveled.

Flat walking. Vertical acceleration oscillates around gravity with peaks at step cadence (1.5 to 2.5 Hz for normal walking). Net vertical acceleration averaged over time is approximately zero.

Threshold-based detection of these three patterns is within reach for a first-year implementation. See Mini-Project 3 for the pattern.

Magnetometer indoors

Kemper Hall has structural steel, electrical wiring, and mounted equipment. All three distort the magnetic field. Do not be surprised if the phone's reported azimuth is 20 to 45 degrees off from true north in parts of the building. This is one of the reasons your indoor position estimate will drift. The error is not your code; it is the physics.

Appendix B: Kemper Hall Evaluation Route

Every team walks the same route at least once during the project. The route:

  1. Start outdoors at a marked location outside Kemper Hall's main entrance. Record your starting coordinates.
  2. Walk into the building through the main entrance.
  3. Take the stairs to the third floor.
  4. Walk to a designated landmark on the third floor (to be announced).
  5. Take the elevator to the first floor.
  6. Walk to a second designated landmark on the first floor.
  7. Exit the building through the main entrance.
  8. Return to the marked starting location outside.

Landmarks will be designated with floor-plan coordinates and visible markers during the project period. Teams report their app's estimated position at each landmark, at the two floor transitions, and at the final outdoor return point.

Appendix D: Timer Pattern for a Live UI

Your app needs to update plots and cloud uploads continuously during a walk. The clean MATLAB pattern is a timer object created in your app's Start callback and stopped in your Stop callback.

% In the Start Activity callback:
app.UpdateTimer = timer( ...
    'ExecutionMode', 'fixedRate', ...
    'Period', 1.0, ...                 % fires once per second
    'TimerFcn', @(~,~) app.updateLoop());
start(app.UpdateTimer);

% In the Stop Activity callback:
stop(app.UpdateTimer);
delete(app.UpdateTimer);

The updateLoop method reads the latest sensor samples, computes derived quantities, updates the plots and summary panel, and (if the reach feature is chosen) pushes a decimated record to ThingSpeak. Keeping updateLoop short matters; if one iteration takes longer than the timer period, updates will pile up.

Consult the MathWorks documentation for timer; the "Callback function execution time" section is particularly relevant.
