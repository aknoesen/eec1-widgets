## Course Description

The course introduces foundational concepts in Electrical and Computer Engineering (ECE) to first-year students. Recognizing ECE's breadth—from device physics and materials to circuits, systems, and machine learning—the curriculum focuses on essential principles for fundamental understanding. The main objective is to contextualize these core concepts, equipping students for advanced, specialized ECE courses.

The curriculum covers key ECE domains, starting with a high-level perspective on using analog-to-digital converters (ADCs), digital-to-analog converters (DACs), and field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) within embedded systems for control and automation. The course explores analog circuit design for signal processing, examines the breadth of communication systems, and builds intuition for frequency analysis, signal processing, and filtering techniques. Finally, these topics are integrated through the lens of systems design and engineering, emphasizing practical, multidisciplinary problem-solving.

This course intentionally distills each concept to its essential foundations, providing students with a broad but coherent understanding that prepares them for subsequent courses in analog and digital electronics, signal processing, and the ECE‑specific mathematical and physical principles encountered in the required math and physics sequences.

## Your Learning Resources & The Role of AI

The textbook, <a href="https://canvas.ucdavis.edu/courses/1072443/files/30492587?wrap=1" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><strong>ECE Confidential: Cracking The Code</strong></a> is your primary learning resource. It provides the conceptual foundation for every principle you will encounter. To succeed, you must move beyond passive reading to active mastery of the text.

### A Flipped Classroom Workflow
Success depends on a consistent weekly rhythm, moving from independent exposure to group reinforcement:

1.  **Build the Foundation:** Complete assigned textbook chapters *before* the Monday lecture.
2.  **Deepen with AI:** Use AI tools (like **NotebookLM** or **Gemini**) to clarify difficult ideas, explore alternative explanations, or generate practice problems.
3.  **Verify Independently:** Complete the **Reading Check** by Monday at Noon without AI to ensure you have internalized core concepts.
4.  **Reinforce in Lab:** Return to the text and AI during physical lab activities to strengthen your grasp of methods and data analysis.

### Using AI Responsibly
You are encouraged to use AI as a sophisticated study companion for "hardest conceptual hurdles" like displacement current or negative feedback. Refer to the <a href="https://canvas.ucdavis.edu/courses/1072443/files/30574892?wrap=1" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ECE Emerge AI-Assisted Coding Guide</a> for more details.

> **Important:** AI supports your understanding but does not replace engagement. You are responsible for the accuracy of your technical work and must properly attribute AI contributions in your projects.

### The Goal: Personal Mastery
The **Final Examination is closed-book**. It is the primary venue where independent understanding is evaluated. Use AI to practice, but ensure your ultimate objective is personal mastery.

## Learning Objectives
Upon successful completion, students will be able to:

* **Fundamental Engineering Concepts:** Apply concepts like charge, current, and voltage in practical contexts and demonstrate the application of complex numbers.
* **Technical Tools Proficiency:** Utilize data acquisition modules (ADCs/DACs) and demonstrate proficiency in MATLAB and Python.
* **Measurement and Analysis Skills:** Perform component measurements, capture electrical signals, and document experimental results professionally.
* **Engineering Context and Integration:** Connect theoretical math/physics concepts to practical engineering scenarios.
* **Professional Skills Development:** Practice technical documentation and time management through structured lab cycles.
* **Responsible AI Usage:** Evaluate appropriate use cases for LLMs, maintain academic integrity, and validate AI-generated technical information.

## ECE Emerge Course Structure (Spring 2026)
The course is organized into 8 modules:

| Module | Lecture Topics | Lab Topics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Module #1 | Foundations and Circuit Language | Intro to ADALM2000 (M2K); Soldering |
| Module #2 | DC Circuits: KCL, KVL, Voltage Dividers | DC Measurements; Series/Parallel |
| Module #3 | Signals in Time and Frequency; Fourier | Device Characterization; Frequency Domain |
| Module #4 | Circuits That Remember: RC and RL | Transient Response; Extracting $\tau$ |

| Module #7 | Applied Op-Amps: Inverting/Summing | Precision Amps; Level Shifting |
| Module #8 | Project | Capstone Work |

## The 12-Hour Weekly Commitment
Following the **Carnegie Rule**, this 4-unit course requires **12 hours of work per week**.

* **In-Class (3 Hours):** 50-minute lecture and 2-hour lab.
* **Independent Mastery (9 Hours):**
    * **Pre-Lecture (~5-6 Hours):** Deep-dive into the textbook and Reading Check.
    * **Pre-Lab (~2 Hours):** Calculations and circuit diagrams.
    * **Synthesis & Analysis (~1-2 Hours):** Data analysis, report writing, and Module Assessment.

### Universal Deadlines
* **Monday Noon:** Reading Checks due before lecture.
* **Tuesday Noon:** Pre-Lab Assignments due before any lab start.
* **Saturday Noon:** Post-Lab Report and Module Assessment due.

> **Why Deadlines Matter:** These prevent a 9-hour weekend crunch and ensure professional equity across all lab sections regardless of the day you attend. **Deadlines are strictly enforced.**
