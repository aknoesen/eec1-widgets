ENG 6: Engineering Problem Solving with Applied AI

Course Orientation: Read Before Lecture 1

Your first Reading Assignment is due before Lecture 1. That is intentional. This document explains why, and what to expect from every week that follows.

1. What You Will Build

By the end of this course you will have built, from scratch, a smartphone-based sensor analysis platform. Not a toy script. A real, working engineering tool.

The five-lab progression:

  1. Stream live accelerometer data from your smartphone into MATLAB.
  2. Compute and visualize the resultant acceleration, detect peak events, and produce publication-quality annotated plots.
  3. Build a collision detection algorithm parameterized by threshold, analogous to an airbag deployment system.
  4. Reorganize everything into an object-oriented class, then estimate your walking displacement by numerically integrating your phone's accelerometer data twice (dead reckoning).
  5. Wrap the entire platform in a graphical user interface that any non-programmer can use.

Every lecture topic and every lab session is a deliberate step toward that final product. Nothing is filler.

The course also integrates generative AI throughout, not as a curiosity, but as a genuine engineering tool you will learn to use responsibly and strategically. You will use AI the way a professional engineer would: to accelerate understanding, not to replace it.

3. How This Course Is Structured: The Flipped Classroom

ENG 6 is a flipped classroom. If you have not taken one before, here is what that means in practice.

In a traditional course, the instructor lectures and you go home to do problem sets. In this course, the sequence is reversed:

  • You encounter the material first — on your own, before lecture, through structured reading assignments in ZyBooks.
  • Lecture time is used for active work: exercises, team activities, and consolidation of the concepts that are hardest to grasp from reading alone.
  • Lab is where you apply everything to real engineering problems with real data from your own smartphone.

This is a design choice, not an accident. Research consistently shows that students who wrestle with material before class, even imperfectly, learn more than students who receive a lecture first and practice later. The discomfort of reading something before you fully understand it is part of the process. Lecture is where that discomfort gets resolved.

The critical implication

You cannot show up to lecture unprepared and catch up by listening.

Lecture is not a re-delivery of the reading. It is built on top of it.

A student who skips the pre-lecture reading will sit in a 90-minute session that makes little sense, fall behind, and find the lab nearly impossible.

A student who arrives prepared will find the lecture clarifies, deepens, and extends what they read.

5. The Reading Arc — How the Workload Shifts

The reading load is front-loaded. This is worth knowing before you start.

5. The Reading Arc — How the Workload Shifts
Weeks Reading Load Primary Emphasis
1–5 Dense. Variables, arrays, indexing, plotting, 2D arrays, branches. Building the mental model. This is where the foundation is laid. Invest here.
6–9 Moderate. OOP, applied math, GUIs, strings, loops. Concepts become more specialized; you are applying an existing foundation.
10–13 Lighter. Structures, advanced visualization, data structures. Application and integration dominate. You are building, not just reading.
14–18 Minimal new reading. Advanced functions, linear algebra. The Group Project is the primary vehicle. You are engineering, not studying.

The analogy is learning to ride a bicycle. Getting on for the first time requires intense focus on balance, steering, and pedaling simultaneously — everything at once. It is cognitively demanding. But once you are moving, riding becomes automatic and you can direct your attention to where you are going.

The first five weeks of this course are getting on the bicycle. The rest of the course is riding.

The students who struggle in the second half are almost always students who did not get solidly on the bicycle in the first half. The early investment protects everything that follows.

How to read this chart:

The red dashed line is the Carnegie out-of-class target for a 4-unit course — 8.2 hours per week. The shaded region (weeks 1–5) is where the bars exceed that line. This is intentional and expected. The early weeks are dense because you are building the programming vocabulary and mental models that every subsequent lab depends on.

From week 6 onward, the bars drop below the Carnegie line as reading becomes lighter and the emphasis shifts to building and applying. The Group Project adds hours back in the final weeks — but of a fundamentally different character. You are engineering something, not studying for it.

The term average across all weeks comes out close to the Carnegie target. The course is calibrated correctly for 4 units. What it is not calibrated for is doing the work unevenly.

A direct word about scheduling

The assignments in this course do not work well if you batch them.

Reading both assignments the morning of Thursday's lecture defeats the purpose of the flipped model. Doing Creative Activities the night before lab defeats their purpose. Skipping Pre-Lab work and hoping to improvise in the lab session defeats the purpose of the lab.

The rhythm works when you follow it as designed: each piece completed before the thing it prepares you for. Block the time in your calendar now — before week one begins.

8. Before You Start : The Five Things That Matter Most

  1. Complete every reading assignment before the lecture it prepares you for.
    Lecture is built on top of the reading. This is non-negotiable.

  2. Schedule the work across the week, not in one sitting.
    The rhythm is designed. Fighting it makes everything harder.

  3. Invest especially in the first five weeks.
    The foundation you build then determines how much you can do in every lab that follows.

  4. Use AI to deepen understanding, not to replace it.
    The evaluations will reveal whether you actually learned the material. Plan accordingly.

  5. Come to lecture prepared to do things, not to hear things.
    Lecture is 90 minutes of consolidation and practice. It only works if you arrive ready.

Students who follow this structure consistently tend to find the course demanding but manageable, nd they tend to arrive at the end with something they are genuinely proud of having built.

Students who do not tend to find the course chaotic and the evaluations alarming.

The difference is almost entirely scheduling and consistency, not talent.

We look forward to seeing what you build.
