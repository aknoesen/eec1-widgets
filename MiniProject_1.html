Mini-Project 1: Introduction to MATLAB Mobile

How This Course Works: Read This First

Every mini-project in ENG6 follows the same three-phase structure. Lab 1 is where you learn that structure, and it will repeat every week.

PRE-LAB  →  LAB SESSION  →  POST-LAB
(before)     (in person)     (after)

Pre-Lab: work you complete before arriving at the lab session. This includes setup, background reading, and a focused AI exploration that prepares you to get the most out of the hands-on work. Pre-lab work is submitted as part of your post-lab package, so keep everything you produce.

Lab Session: the in-person period where you do the hands-on work: connecting sensors, collecting data, running scripts, generating plots. This is where TAs are available to help. Some tasks in this course can only be done in the lab session; you need your phone, a working MATLAB connection, and physical space to move around. Come prepared.

Post-Lab: after the session, you complete your submission: uploading plots, answering structured questions, and writing your formal reflection. The post-lab submission is only possible if you documented your work during the lab session. If you leave the lab without your plots saved and your observations noted, you will not be able to complete the submission.

Documentation Is Not Optional

Engineering work is only useful if it is recorded. In industry, an undocumented experiment might as well not have happened. In this course, the same principle applies practically: if you did not save it during the lab, you cannot submit it after.

During every lab session, you are responsible for:
- Saving every plot generated as a PNG file before moving on
- Adding your name to every plot title
- Writing down key numerical observations (values, axis labels, anything you will need to describe later) in a notes document or on paper
- Submitting your code to MATLAB Grader before you leave, and TAs can help you debug on the spot

Think of your documentation as a record you are writing for your future self, who will be sitting down to complete the post-lab submission a day later with no TA present.

Goals

By the end of this mini-project you will have:
- Connected your smartphone to MATLAB and streamed live accelerometer data
- Written your first MATLAB data logging script
- Identified the X, Y, and Z axes of your phone's accelerometer through physical experimentation
- Discovered and explained what a stationary phone's accelerometer actually measures, a result that will matter in every lab that follows

Pre-Lab 1b: AI Exploration

Before working with a sensor, it helps to understand what it actually measures. Smartphone accelerometers are commonly misunderstood: the name suggests they measure acceleration in the everyday sense, but the physical principle is more specific than that.

Complete the following AI exploration before your lab session and keep a written record of what you learn.

Step 1: Open an AI assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or similar).

Step 2: Use the following as your opening prompt. You do not need to use it word for word, but it should reflect your actual starting point:

"I am a first-year engineering student about to use a smartphone accelerometer in a lab for the first time. I know that acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. What I do not understand is: what physical quantity does a smartphone accelerometer actually measure, and how does it work mechanically? Please explain the sensor itself, specifically what is happening inside the device, not just the formula it outputs."

Step 3: Continue the conversation with follow-up questions until you can answer these in your own words:
- What type of physical mechanism is inside a MEMS accelerometer?
- What does the sensor detect directly: force, motion, or something else?
- How does the output relate to the acceleration of the device?

These three questions correspond directly to Pre-Lab deliverables 2(a) and 2(b) in the GradeBridge submission.

Step 4: Write down your answers in a notes document, in your own words, not copied from the AI.

Step 5: Write a 3–5 sentence summary of what you learned about how the sensor works. Keep this; it becomes Pre-Lab deliverable 2(c) and the starting point for Post-Lab Problem 1(a).

Note: Step 3.2 of the lab session will produce a result you may not expect. Your pre-lab work here gives you the physical understanding needed to reason about that result when you see it, not before you see it.

Professional Practice: Read the Documentation First

Throughout this course, you will see links to the official MathWorks documentation whenever a new MATLAB function is introduced. This is intentional. In professional engineering practice, reading the documentation is the first step when you encounter an unfamiliar function, not a last resort.

MathWorks documentation pages follow a consistent format: a brief description of what the function does, the full syntax showing all input and output arguments, a description of each argument, and, most usefully, worked examples at the bottom of the page that show the function being used in realistic contexts. These examples are often the fastest way to understand how a function should be called.

The habit to build: When you see a documentation link in this course, open it. Scan the syntax. Look at an example. Then return here and continue. This takes two minutes and will save you far more time than guessing or immediately turning to an AI tool.

AI assistants can be genuinely useful; this course makes deliberate use of them, but they are most useful as a follow-up when you have a specific question the documentation did not answer. Reaching for AI before reading the documentation skips the step that builds real understanding of the tools you are using.

The link below is your first example. Click it, spend two minutes with it, then come back.

In the MATLAB Online command window, type the following commands. The first line creates a mobiledev object, which is MATLAB's connection to the sensors on your phone.

m = mobiledev
m.Logging = 1

Wave your phone around in front of you for a few seconds. Then type:

m.Logging = 0

To inspect the raw data, use accellog to retrieve the logged values from your mobiledev object:

accellog(m)

You should see a 2D array of numbers. Each row is one time sample. Columns 1, 2, and 3 are the measured acceleration in the X, Y, and Z directions.

If you see an empty array or an error: Check that MATLAB Mobile is open and in the foreground on your phone, that Sensor Access is toggled on, and that your phone and laptop are connected to the internet. Ask a TA if the issue persists.

Lab Step 2: Write the Logging Script

Typing commands one at a time in the command window is tedious and error-prone. A script is a text file containing a sequence of MATLAB commands that runs all at once. You will write all your code as scripts from now on.

Click New Script in MATLAB Online to open the script editor.

Write a script that performs the following steps in order:

  1. Creates a connection to MATLAB Mobile and assigns it to a variable named m
  2. Starts sensor logging
  3. Collects data for exactly 10 seconds
  4. Stops sensor logging
  5. Calls plotAccelerometer to generate and save a plot of the collected data

Hints:
- Use pause(10) to make MATLAB wait for 10 seconds before stopping (pause docs)
- plotAccelerometer is a script provided on Canvas. Download it and place it in the same folder as your script before running
- Save your script as exactly logging.m; this filename is required by the autograder

Organizing your files:
As your projects grow in complexity, keeping files organized becomes essential. Create the following folder structure in MATLAB Drive:

MATLAB Drive
└── ENG6
    └── MiniProject1
        ├── logging.m
        └── plotAccelerometer.m

Set your MATLAB working directory to MiniProject1 using the folder path bar at the top of the MATLAB window before running your script.

Run your script. If it produces errors, read the error message carefully; MATLAB error messages tell you the line number and the nature of the problem. Ask a TA if you are stuck.

Lab Step 3: Visualizing and Interpreting Sensor Readings

Raw numbers are hard to interpret. Producing clear, informative graphics from data is one of the most important skills in engineering. In this step you will generate and interpret two sets of plots.

Lab Step 3.1: Identifying Your Phone's Axes

The accelerometer reports acceleration in three directions labeled X, Y, and Z, but which physical direction on your phone is X? Which is Y? Which is Z?

Your task is to figure this out experimentally. Do not look it up , the point of this step is to develop the skill of reading sensor data to infer physical meaning.

Procedure:

Hold your phone directly in front of you with the screen facing you. Run a logging session while moving your phone along one direction at a time:
- Up and down
- Left and right
- Toward you and away from you (in and out)

Use swift, large movements so the signal is clearly visible in the plot. Try to isolate one direction at a time as cleanly as possible; slow down between movements.

Run plotAccelerometer after collecting data to examine the output. Repeat with different movements until you are confident about which axis is which.

Document as you go:
- When you identify an axis, note it down immediately
- Save each plot as a PNG using sgtitle to add your name as a title spanning all panels before saving:
matlab % Example: add this before saving sgtitle('Accelerometer Readings — Your Name Here')
- You do not need to save every plot from this exploration; save only one clean final plot showing the movements that revealed each axis

Once you have identified all three axes, generate one clean plot that shows the movements you used to identify them. Save it as a PNG to your MATLAB Drive. You will circle the identifying features and prepare it for submission during the post-lab phase.

Phone sketch:

Draw a front-view sketch of your phone with all three axes labeled, including positive and negative directions. Use the template below as a guide; your version must have all six directions filled in.

Lab Step 3.2: Stationary Phone Measurement

Procedure:

Place your phone face up on a flat, stationary surface. Do not touch it. Run a 10-second logging session. Run plotAccelerometer to generate the plot.

Observe and record the following in your notes document right now, while you are looking at the plot:

  1. Which axis shows the largest reading? Write down the axis letter.
  2. What is the approximate value on that axis, in m/s²? Write down the number.
  3. What are the approximate readings on the other two axes?
  4. What did you expect to see on a completely stationary phone? Does the result match that expectation?

In-Lab AI Exploration:

You have now seen the result. Open an AI assistant and use the following as your prompt:

"I placed my smartphone completely still on a flat table and ran the accelerometer. One axis showed a reading of approximately 9.8 m/s²; the other two were close to zero. The phone was not moving at all. Please explain the physical reason for the non-zero reading. Why does a stationary phone produce a non-zero accelerometer output?"

Continue the conversation until you can answer in your own words:
- What physical force is the sensor detecting?
- Why does that force appear in the measurement even when the phone is not accelerating?
- What would happen to the readings if you tilted the phone at 45 degrees?

Write your answers in your notes document. These answers, together with your observation, are the material for your formal reflection.

How to annotate your saved plot

Option 1: Digital Markup (Recommended)

This method preserves image quality and does not require printing.

  • Windows: Open your PNG in Paint or the Photos app. Use the "Edit and Create" or "Draw" tool to circle your motifs, and the "Text" tool to confirm your name is visible in the title area.
  • Mac: Open the PNG in Preview. Click the Show Markup Toolbar button (the pencil icon). Use the "Shapes" tool to circle features and the "Text" tool to add your name if needed.
  • Tablet or iPad: Use the native Markup feature in your photo gallery to draw directly on the image with a stylus or finger.

Option 2: Physical Markup (Print and Photograph)

If you prefer working by hand:

  1. Print your saved PNG.
  2. Write your name in the title area if it is not already visible, and circle the identifying motif on each of the three panels (X, Y, and Z) using a pen or highlighter.
  3. Photograph the annotated printout from directly above with your phone camera, in good lighting.
  4. If the photo file exceeds 500 KB, take a screenshot of it or use a resize tool to reduce the file size before uploading.

Requirements:
- Your name in the plot title
- The motif (feature) you used to identify each axis direction is circled or highlighted
- Three panels visible (X, Y, Z) with time on the horizontal axis

Problem 5(a): Formal Reflection (AI-graded)

Write a reflection of 150–200 words as continuous prose, not a bulleted list, not three separate paragraphs, but a single coherent argument.

Your reflection must accomplish three things, in this order:

The Technical Why: Explain the physical mechanism. Name the force the sensor is detecting and explain why it appears even when the phone is not moving.

Grading note: Naming the correct value (9.8 m/s²) without explaining the mechanism will not satisfy The Technical Why. Writing "this will be important in future labs" without specifying how will not satisfy The Lab Application. Your reflection will be evaluated on whether each point is present and substantive.
