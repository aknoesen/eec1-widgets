Module 3: Signals in Time and Frequency

Module 3 shifts from constant DC values to signals that change over time. You will learn two equally valid and complementary ways to describe any signal: the time domain and the frequency domain.

This module covers:
* Amplitude, frequency, period, and phase of sinusoidal signals
* RMS amplitude and why it is the right measure for power calculations
* Fourier synthesis: how complex waveforms are built from sinusoids
* The Nyquist criterion and the consequences of aliasing

The Sinusoid: Nature's Building Block

The sinusoid is the fundamental building block of all periodic signals. You will master the parameters that define any sine wave and learn why RMS amplitude is the standard measure for power calculations, representing the DC equivalent that delivers the same power as the AC signal.

Fourier Synthesis

The core conceptual leap of this module is the Fourier Series, approached from a synthesis-first perspective. Rather than "breaking apart" a complex waveform, you will see it as built from a specific recipe of sinusoids. Square waves, triangle waves, and sawtooth waves each have a characteristic harmonic recipe that explains their shape.

A common misconception is that Fourier analysis takes a signal apart. The synthesis-first view corrects this: the sinusoidal components were always there. The Fourier Series reveals what was already present.

The Nyquist Criterion

Because the M2K is a digital instrument, it samples signals at a finite rate. To capture a signal faithfully, you must sample it at least twice as fast as its highest frequency. Failing to do so produces aliasing, where high-frequency content masquerades as a lower frequency. Understanding this limit is essential for interpreting any measurement made with digital instruments.
