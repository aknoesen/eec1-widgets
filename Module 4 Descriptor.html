Module 4: Circuits That Remember

Module 4 introduces the two energy-storage elements in electronics: capacitors and inductors. Unlike resistors, these components remember their prior state, giving circuits the ability to respond to changes over time.

This module covers:
* How capacitors store energy in electric fields and resist instantaneous voltage change
* How inductors store energy in magnetic fields and resist instantaneous current change
* The time constant and the exponential step response of RC and RL circuits
* Engineering judgment about when a small physical detail matters

Energy Storage and Fundamental Constraints

Capacitors and inductors each store energy in a different form. A capacitor stores energy in an electric field between its plates; its defining constraint is that voltage cannot change instantaneously. An inductor stores energy in the magnetic field around its coil; its defining constraint is that current cannot change instantaneously. These constraints are what give circuits memory.

The Time Constant

When a switch changes state in an RC or RL circuit, the response does not jump to its new value instantly. Instead, it follows an exponential curve governed by the time constant. For an RC circuit, the time constant is the product of resistance and capacitance. For an RL circuit, it is the ratio of inductance to resistance. After one time constant, a charging capacitor has reached 63.2% of its final voltage.

Engineering Judgment

A practical theme of this module is learning when a small physical detail is significant. The M2K signal generator has an internal resistance of about 50 ohms. You will calculate and verify why this resistance is negligible in the RC circuit but significant in the RL circuit. Knowing what to include and what to safely ignore is a core engineering skill.
