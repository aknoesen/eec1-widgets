Module 5: Phasors, Impedance, and Filters

Module 5 is the conceptual bridge of the course. You will combine what you know about DC circuits, signals, and energy storage to analyze circuits driven by AC signals, and you will see how a simple arrangement of resistors and capacitors can selectively pass or block specific frequencies.

This module covers:
* Phasors: representing sinusoidal signals as complex numbers to make AC analysis algebraic
* Impedance: the frequency-dependent generalization of resistance
* Low-pass, high-pass, and other filter types built from resistors and capacitors
* The cutoff frequency and the decibel scale

Phasors: Making AC Analysis Algebraic

Analyzing circuits with time-varying signals normally requires solving differential equations. Phasors let you sidestep the calculus. A phasor encodes a signal's amplitude and phase as a single complex number. The key geometric insight is that multiplying by the imaginary unit j rotates a phasor by 90 degrees, which is exactly how a capacitor shifts the phase of a signal passing through it.

Impedance: Ohm's Law for AC

Impedance generalizes resistance to AC circuits. Like resistance, impedances combine in series and parallel by the same rules you learned in Module 2. Unlike resistance, impedance depends on frequency. A capacitor's impedance falls as frequency rises. An inductor's impedance rises with frequency. This frequency dependence is what makes filtering possible.

The voltage divider from Module 2 becomes a filter the moment you replace one resistor with a capacitor. The circuit structure is identical; only the frequency dependence of the impedance is new.

Filters

By combining a resistor and a capacitor as an impedance voltage divider, you create a circuit whose gain changes with frequency. A low-pass filter passes low frequencies and attenuates high ones. A high-pass filter does the opposite. The cutoff frequency marks the boundary between the passband and the stopband, defined as the point where output power is reduced by half.
