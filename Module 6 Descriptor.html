Module 6: Operational Amplifiers

Module 6 introduces the operational amplifier, one of the most widely used components in analog electronics. You will learn how to tame a device with enormous open-loop gain using negative feedback, and how to build precision amplifiers whose gain is set entirely by external resistors.

This module covers:
* The ideal op-amp assumptions and the concept of negative feedback
* The Golden Rules: virtual short and virtual open
* Voltage follower, inverting amplifier, and non-inverting amplifier circuits
* Real-world limits: slew rate and output saturation

The Power of Negative Feedback

An op-amp on its own has an open-loop gain so large that almost any input signal drives the output to its maximum or minimum. Negative feedback changes everything. By connecting the output back to the inverting input, the circuit forces the output to settle at a value where the two inputs are nearly equal. This self-correcting behavior makes the op-amp stable, linear, and predictable.

The Golden Rules

When negative feedback is present and the op-amp is behaving ideally, two rules let you analyze almost any circuit without knowing anything about the op-amp's internal design. The virtual short rule states that the op-amp drives its output to force the two input voltages to be equal. The virtual open rule states that no current flows into either input terminal. These rules are the distilled consequence of ideal negative feedback, not definitions to memorize but results to understand.

Amplifier Circuits

With the Golden Rules in hand, you can analyze a family of precision amplifiers. The voltage follower provides unity gain with high input impedance, solving the loading problem you encountered in Module 2. The non-inverting amplifier provides gain greater than one while preserving the signal's polarity. The inverting amplifier scales and inverts the signal, with gain set by the ratio of two external resistors.
