About ThingSpeak (brief background)

Both tracks use ThingSpeak, and none of you has seen it before in this course. Here is a short primer so the name is not unfamiliar when you hit the project docs.

What it is. ThingSpeak is a cloud platform built by MathWorks (the same company that makes MATLAB). In plain terms, it is a place on the internet where your MATLAB app can store numbers and any other computer (including your own later, or a teammate's) can read them back. Each team will get its own ThingSpeak "channel," which is essentially a table in the cloud with up to eight numbered columns that you decide how to use.

Why both tracks need it. Your MATLAB app runs on one computer at a time, but both tracks need data to survive beyond a single session or be shared across machines. ThingSpeak is the bridge.

  • Track A uses its channel to store a summary of each walk: position, floor, speed, regime (outdoor vs. indoor), and so on. You upload the summary at the end of each walk; later you load past walks from the channel and replay them in the app.
  • Track B uses its channel as a message bus between players' computers. When one player takes a turn, the move is written to the channel; the other player's app checks the channel on a timer, reads the new move, and updates the game state. This is how turn passing works across machines on different networks.

What you need to learn right now. Nothing. The Week 1 workshop (see below) teaches ThingSpeak from scratch.

Teams of Three

You will work in teams of three. Teams may form across lab sections. You may only participate in one team.

Each team member takes on one of three complementary area roles. Each role is a specialized focus that integrates into the team's project. Every role carries equal weight.

Role 1: Cloud & Data Layer

Owns the team's ThingSpeak integration. This includes designing the channel layout, writing and reading data, handling cloud rate limits, and making sure the team's data flows reliably between the app and the cloud. If you like systems thinking, networking, and clean data management, this is your role.

Role 2: App Designer & Live UI

Owns the team's graphical interface and the live view that updates during activity. For Track A this is the map and floor plan integration; for Track B this is the game board, score panel, and turn indicator. If you like making things visual and interactive, this is your role.

Role 3: Sensor Signal Processing & Event Detection

Owns the team's algorithms for detecting events from phone sensors. For Track A this is stair climb, elevator, and step detection; for Track B this is gesture detection (shake, tilt, flick). If you like signal analysis and tuning detection algorithms, this is your role.

Each role has a dedicated TA leading it. Rishad Joarder covers Cloud & Data Layer, Elnaz Hamdarsi covers App Designer & Live UI, and Basheer Ammar covers Sensor Signal Processing & Event Detection. Each TA runs workshops and holds consultation hours in their area. The designated role holder on your team is your team's liaison to that TA.

Important: The role is about ownership and expertise, not exclusion. Every team member attends every workshop. Every team member reads and contributes to every file. The role holder is the team's designated integrator and the person who goes deepest in that area.

Collective Responsibilities

Regardless of which track you choose, your team will be collectively responsible for delivering (to be finalized):

  • A working MATLAB App Designer app plus supporting classes
  • A team ThingSpeak channel URL with real data
  • A short PDF (2 to 3 pages): an error characterization report for Track A, or a design memo for Track B
  • A 6-minute unlisted YouTube video following a specified format
  • A team self-assessment form
  • Two peer review forms of other teams' videos (assigned to you after submission)
  • A confidential teammate evaluation form completed by each team member individually (see below)

Grading is team-based. All three team members receive the same grade unless there is a significant imbalance that is flagged during review. The teammate evaluation form is the primary input that surfaces imbalances. Detailed rubric information is in the draft project documents linked above.

Teammate Evaluation Form (End of Project)

At the end of the project, each team member will independently complete a short confidential evaluation of their teammates. The form is reviewed only by the instructor. It is used to identify significant contribution imbalances within a team that may warrant individual grade adjustments away from the team grade.

For each of your teammates, you will rate the following six statements:

Teammate Evaluation Form (End of Project)
# Statement
1 This teammate actively contributed to discussions and encouraged input from others.
2 They took on leadership responsibilities when needed.
3 They respected others' ideas and helped build consensus.
4 They completed assigned tasks on time and with care.
5 They helped the team stay focused and achieve lab objectives.
6 I would want to work with this teammate again in future labs.

This is a different artifact from the "two peer review forms of other teams' videos" listed above. The peer review forms evaluate other teams' work; the teammate evaluation form evaluates your own teammates' contributions. Both are required.

Knowing about this form upfront is intentional. It is easier to develop good team-work habits from week 1 than to fix them at the end. If something is going wrong inside your team during the project, raise it with your team or with a TA early; do not wait until the form to surface it.

Team Declaration: Action Required

You will receive a short survey that does two things: it declares your team, and it captures your team's ranking of the two project tracks. Here is how it works:

  1. Form your team of three. Confirm verbally that all three of you are committed.
  2. Each team member submits the survey individually, listing the names of the other two members.
  3. The three matching submissions confirm the team. This triple confirmation prevents miscommunication (for example, Alice listing Bob and Carol while Carol lists Dan and Eve).
  4. Rank the two tracks in your preferred order. You will most likely get your first choice; your second choice may be assigned if needed to balance team counts across tracks.

Deadline: Monday, May 4. Because you can only participate in one team, think about team formation seriously before committing. Once declared, changing teams will be disruptive for everyone involved.

Recommendations for Team Formation

  • Pick people you can work with on a project that will likely involve testing outside of class. Track A requires physical walks through Kemper Hall. Track B requires coordinated testing across two machines.
  • Read both draft documents (linked above) and discuss which track appeals to you before committing. It is easier to commit together than to negotiate afterward.
  • Distribute the three area roles among team members based on interest and strength. No role is more important than another; each carries equal weight in the project's scope and in grading.
